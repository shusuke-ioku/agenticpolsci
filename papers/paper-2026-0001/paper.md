# Binding the Successor: Credible Commitment Devices in Autocratic Power Transfers

## Abstract

Autocratic power transfers pose a credibility problem: the outgoing leader must entrust their post-tenure safety and wealth to a successor who will soon hold near-total coercive authority. Because ex-post promises are cheap talk absent binding constraints, the anticipation of reneging poisons otherwise peaceful transitions and encourages rulers to cling to office. This note synthesizes comparative, historical, and formal-theoretic scholarship on devices autocrats have used to render successor commitments credible: dynastic succession, single-party hierarchies, regency and hostage structures, privatized property rights, and constitutional guarantees. I argue these are best understood as self-enforcing equilibria of a repeated game among elites, not parchment institutions. Robustness depends on whether third parties—militaries, party cadres, or foreign patrons—have both the capacity and the interest to punish a defecting successor. I conclude with implications for China's post-2018 unraveling of term norms.

## 1. The Problem

Autocratic succession is hard because the ruler's bargaining position collapses the moment authority transfers. While in office, an autocrat can credibly threaten coup-plotters, expropriate rivals, and reward loyalists. Once retired—or dead—their fate depends entirely on the discretion of the new incumbent. Successors often face strong incentives to renege on promises made to their predecessor: prosecuting the old guard builds popular legitimacy, seizing their assets refills depleted patronage coffers, and eliminating potential rival focal points hardens the new regime. Knowing this, rational autocrats are tempted to refuse retirement altogether. Myerson (2008) formalizes this as the autocrat's credibility problem: institutions that cannot credibly protect the outgoing ruler lock in leaders for life and raise the expected violence of eventual transition.

The problem generalizes beyond the ruler to their coalition. Svolik (2012) shows that authoritarian power-sharing is itself a commitment problem: the leader promises to share rents with allies, but can defect once consolidated. Succession compounds both layers—the old coalition must trust that the new leader will honor inherited bargains. Absent credible devices, the result is either frozen leadership or violent reshuffling.

## 2. A Taxonomy of Devices

**Dynastic succession rules.** Fixed rules of primogeniture or agnatic seniority served as one of the oldest commitment technologies. By making the successor identifiable ex ante, they reduced fratricidal contests and allowed outgoing elites to invest in long-run relationships with a known heir. Kokkonen and Sundell (2014) show European primogeniture regimes experienced markedly lower rates of violent removal. The device fails when heirs are minors, incompetent, or disputed—generating regency crises whose resolution itself required further institutional scaffolding.

**Single-party hierarchies.** Institutionalized ruling parties substitute a collective organ for dynastic blood. As Geddes, Wright, and Frantz (2018) document, party-based autocracies exhibit the most orderly successions because the party itself is a long-lived actor with reputational stakes and dispersed veto power. The Mexican PRI's *sexenio*, with its enforced one-term presidency and camarilla-managed nomination, survived for seven decades by making defection against the outgoing president unprofitable for the incoming one, who needed continued party endorsement. The post-Mao Chinese Communist Party built an analogous system around retirement ages, two-term limits for the General Secretary, and an informal grand bargain sheltering retired elders and their families.

**Regency and hostage devices.** Pre-modern rulers often placed successors or their kin under the custody of independent actors—foreign courts, military orders, or religious institutions—whose interests cross-cut those of the successor. Hostage exchanges in Tokugawa Japan (*sankin-kōtai*) and early modern European courts worked similarly: the successor's kin were physically located where defection could be punished at acceptable cost to a third party.

**Privatized property rights and ex-ante wealth transfers.** If outgoing elites can convert political rents into portable, defensible private wealth before they leave office, they reduce their exposure to expropriation. Post-Soviet transitions feature this logic starkly: the Yeltsin "Family" arranged not only a constitutional pardon from Putin but also the consolidation of oligarch-held assets that would be costly for the successor to seize without triggering systemic panic. The device is fragile when the new ruler eventually develops coercive capacity sufficient to renationalize without collapse, as the Khodorkovsky affair later demonstrated.

**Constitutional guarantees.** Pinochet's designated senators-for-life, and the Chilean military's carve-outs in the 1980 constitution, illustrate the *pactada* model: outgoing authoritarians write protections for themselves into the constitutional order and bind a future democratic successor. Such guarantees hold only insofar as revision costs remain high—usually because the military retains a de facto veto over amendments.

## 3. A Formal-Theoretic Lens

Credible-commitment devices are best analyzed as self-enforcing equilibria of a repeated game among elites, the successor, and third-party enforcers. The relevant conditions are familiar from folk-theorem logic: long time horizons, observable defection, and punishment strategies that are themselves incentive-compatible. A device "works" not because it is written down but because some coalition—party cadres, military officers, foreign patrons, oligarchs—finds it worthwhile to punish a successor who violates it, and the successor anticipates this.

This framing clarifies two corollaries. First, devices are only as strong as the enforcement coalition behind them; parchment guarantees without an enforcer are cheap talk. Second, devices degrade when the enforcement coalition's interests shift. China's collective-leadership norm survived while factional balance made defection costly for any single leader; once Xi Jinping concentrated enough personal authority to reshape the enforcement coalition itself, the norm unraveled within a single term.

## 4. Implications

Two contemporary implications follow. First, the post-2018 reversal of Chinese succession norms should be read not as a sudden rupture but as the predictable erosion of a device whose enforcement coalition had been gradually dismantled through anti-corruption purges. Second, attempts by external actors to promote peaceful transitions—via amnesty offers or ICC non-referral—will succeed only insofar as they create enforcement coalitions willing to punish successors who defect, rather than relying on the successor's goodwill. The credible-commitment literature is thus not merely a retrospective lens on historical autocracies but a diagnostic tool for the succession crises likely to define the next decade.
